# Building Is the Easy Part Now | Mike Krieger on What AI Changed

> **Source:** YouTube | **Author:** Every | **Duration:** 2910
> **URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=KRv9GpJYrUA

## TL;DR

Mike Krieger, co-founder of Instagram and now at Anthropic Labs, discusses how AI has fundamentally changed software development by making the actual coding trivial. However, he emphasizes that building successful products still requires the "taste" and intuition to edit down features, as AI tends to additive rather than reductive. He also explores the shifting paradigm of rewrites and the emergence of personalized, "shadow org chart" AI agents in the workplace.

---

## Key Insights

### The Shift from Adding to Editing

**Main Point:** AI makes building features frictionless, but the true challenge is now editing and knowing what to cut out.

- AI models are excellent at adding features, taking a product from zero to full functionality in hours.
- However, AI lacks the intuition to simplify or cut features, which is essential for a focused, breakout consumer product.
- True product intuition is built over time through real-world usage and incremental decisions.
- Over-complicating a product (like Instagram's predecessor, Bourbon) is a common trap that AI can exacerbate if not carefully managed.

> "The models today are good at adding features. They're not necessarily good about figuring out what to cut out of the product."

### The New Economics of Rewrites

**Main Point:** Rewriting software is no longer the existential risk it once was, thanks to AI.

- The classic "mythical man-month" advice to avoid rewriting systems is less absolute now.
- AI can help diff and ensure feature parity between versions, drastically reducing the time and risk of a rewrite.
- Rewrites that used to take years (like the famous Netscape rewrite) and could kill a company might now only take days.

### The Rise of Specialized Agents

**Main Point:** Personalized AI agents can become specialized delegates within an organization, creating a "shadow org chart."

- Moving away from a single, generic agent toward specialized, personalized agents (like "shadowing" a specific team member's expertise).
- If you trust a colleague for a specific task, you might transfer that trust to their personalized agent.
- Keeping the agent "run loop" open makes it feel more like a collaborator than a tool.
